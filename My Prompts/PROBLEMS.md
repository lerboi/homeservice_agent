Here are the top 5 objections I've found across Reddit threads and industry research — with the counter you can flip on your landing page:

───

1. "It sounds robotic and my customers will hate it"
The most common fear. Reddit threads are full of "people don't like talking to AI they can tell it's AI" and "I had 2 AI receptionists and people hated them." The sample size concern: early bad actors ruined the reputation for everyone.

✅ Counter: Modern voice AI in 2025 is nothing like 2020. Blind tests show 85–95% of callers can't distinguish advanced AI voices from a real human receptionist. The real risk isn't an AI voice — it's a voicemail that guarantees that customer calls your competitor instead.

───

2. "I don't trust it to get the job details right"
Contractors fear AI will hallucinate pricing, mess up scheduling, or lose leads entirely. One HVAC tech on Reddit put it plainly: "It is an algorithm that churns out a 'most likely' result, and even expensive enterprise-level algorithms will fabricate an incorrect answer." For complex jobs, bad info = bad outcomes.

✅ Counter: AI doesn't replace your judgment — it captures the lead so you can make the call. Route complex or sensitive calls to you; let AI handle the 80% of routine bookings, pricing questions, and appointment setting. The hybrid model (AI + human backup) is exactly what companies like Pink Callers run. It's not AI vs. humans — it's AI handling the mundane so you handle the meaningful.

───

3. "It's too expensive"
Ironically, this one cuts against them. Most contractors compare AI to the cost of doing nothing (voicemail = losing 74% of callers), not to a human receptionist. A full-time human receptionist runs $4,000+/month. Live answering services charge $300–$800/month with per-call or per-minute overages that add up fast. AI starts at $99–$299/month flat rate.

✅ Counter: The question isn't "can I afford AI?" It's "can I afford to keep losing $260,400/year to missed calls? (Yes, that's the real number for a contractor getting 42 calls/month at a 74% miss rate.) Even one extra job booked per week pays for the service.

───

4. "I run a specific type of business and AI won't understand my trades"
Plumbers, HVAC techs, and electricians worry that generic AI won't know the difference between a tankless water heater and a standard tank, or understand trade-specific terminology. Reddit commenter in r/microsaas: "These businesses just don't want to innovate... they don't even seem motivated to make more money if it means changing how they operate."

✅ Counter: Voco is trained on your trade. The AI knows plumbing terminology, HVAC diagnostics, electrical codes — and when it doesn't know something, it escalates to you. The key is trade-specific training and a knowledge base you control, not a one-size-fits-all bot.

───

5. "I'm not tech-savvy / it's too complicated to set up"
"I have wanted to try and set one up for my biz but I'm not sure I trust it not to fuck up." This is pure fear of change, not reality. Most modern AI receptionist platforms are plug-and-play with existing phones, calendars, and CRMs.

✅ Counter: Setup takes minutes, not weeks. It plugs into your existing phone line — no new hardware, no IT team. If you can set up a voicemail, you can set up Voco. And if anything ever goes wrong, you have a real human team backing it up.

───

Bonus — the underlying objection nobody says out loud:

"I don't want to change how I've always done things." This is the real one. Home service is a relationship business built on personal trust. Many contractors don't want a machine touching their customer interactions — even if that machine would save them money. Your landing page needs to speak to the owner's identity, not just features. Frame it as: your receptionist, just available 24/7 when you can't be.
